Background

About the DGTP Interoperability Platform

The Government of Sint Maarten is modernising how citizens and businesses deal with the state through the Digital Government Transformation Project (DGTP). At its centre is the D2.2D Interoperability Platform — a shared digital backbone that lets government services work together and draw on the country’s authoritative registries.

Today a citizen renewing a National ID, registering a business or applying for a permit often visits a counter, re-enters the same details, and hands over documents the government already holds. The platform changes that. It connects the source registries — civil registry, land (Kadaster), VROMI, business, UBO and others — through a single Common Message Format, so a service reads verified data once and reuses it (the once-only principle) instead of collecting it again. Citizens sign in with the national eID, every request passes eight security gates, work is orchestrated by a workflow engine, the platform runs on Microsoft Azure, and every action is written to an immutable audit trail.

The intended result is measurable: faster processing, fewer in-person visits, less duplication, and services the Government can trust and monitor independently. KPI Atlas is the interactive companion to the KPI framework that measures all of that — prepared for the World Bank, GovXSM and the Government stakeholders (DLT, NRPB, NRD), together with the OrangeBD delivery team, as a shared reference for the D2.2D KPI meeting and SLA finalisation.

For the D2.2D KPI meeting · the four points raised

Clarifications on the record

▾
  • Operational and Programme Outcome KPIs measure different things. Operational KPIs show whether a service runs well day to day — processing time, backlog, throughput. Programme Outcome KPIs show whether the transformation is landing — digital take-up, completion, satisfaction, once-only reuse. They are grouped separately below.
  • Vendor-enabled tracking is a capability, not a benefit. Online volumes, processing times and API failure rates sit here to prove the platform can produce reliable data — dashboards, exports and monitoring. They show the Government can track the other categories independently; they are not themselves a programme benefit.
  • Vendor KPIs are kept separate from programme success. Platform uptime and incident-SLA compliance measure how the vendor performs against the contract, not whether DGTP succeeds. They sit in the Vendor Operational group and are reviewed through the SLA / contract pack.
  • Every Programme Outcome KPI links to a PDO. Each one carries a line mapping it to a Project Development Objective theme — access and adoption, service efficiency, user experience, once-only and interoperability, or channel shift.
  • To confirm — SLA compliance target: 90% or 95%? The email says 90% of tickets within SLA; the framework says at least 95% for P1 and P2 incidents. One figure should be agreed as the contractual target.
  • To confirm — change failure rate: under 5% or 5–10%? The DevSecOps register says under 5%; the matrix says 5–10%. One target should be agreed (the DORA elite band is about 5%).

Composite SLA — why the platform uptime SLO is 99.5–99.9%, not 99.95%

When services chain, effective availability is the product of their SLAs. A request across Front Door → App Service → API Management → Azure SQL inherits ≈ 99.99% × 99.95% × 99.95% × 99.99% = ~99.88%. The uptime SLO is therefore set at 99.5–99.9% rather than any single service tier, with redundancy (multi-AZ, ZRS/GRS, DR region, independent fallback paths) recovering the gap. SLA figures are Microsoft published values as of mid-2026; confirm the exact tier and region SLA at contract signing.

Standing exclusions & measurement rules (apply to all contractual rows)
  • Scope: production only, within the agreed service window; approved maintenance and external-dependency outages excluded and attributed separately.
  • Time-based metrics state calendar vs business hours and pause rules (applicant action, external dependency, statutory waits).
  • Availability & incident measures use an independent monitoring source; latency stated at percentiles (P95), not averages.
  • A metric becomes a contractual KPI only with defined scope, data source, exclusions, calculation, reporting period, dispute process and consequence of non-compliance.
